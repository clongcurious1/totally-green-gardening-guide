Pests, Predators, and Pollinators

Creating a Totally Green garden is not for the faint of heart.

Look closely at the bugs in the soil, on your plants, and in the air.

Pollinators and predators are your new best friends.

Nobody likes a pest.

APHID

Pest

Aphids attack vegetables, legumes, stone fruit crops, roses, apples, and ornamentals.

Aphids quickly invade a garden as females are born capable of birthing new aphids within 10 days.

Non-toxic aphid control measures include spraying affected plants with a hose and planting companion plants that attract beneficial predators.

BUTTERFLY

Pollinator

Butterflies distribute pollen as they move from flower to flower in search of nectar.

Support butterflies by planting nectar-rich flowers like monch aster, butterfly bush, coneflower, sedum, and zinnia.

Larval host plants include ironweed, milkweed, hollyhock, and mallow.

CUTWORM

Pest

Cutworms (larval moths) feed (mostly at dusk) on vegetables and flowers, targeting young seedlings and transplants.

Non-toxic methods of controlling cutworms include aluminum foil plant “collars” at the base of new transplants and planting companion plants to attract beneficial insects (lacewings, parasitic wasps).

HONEYBEE

Pollinator

Honeybees are super-important pollinators for flowers, fruits, and vegetables.

Support Honeybees by planting flowers and herbs – like lavender and bluebells - that produce nectar.

LACEWING

Predator

Green lacewing larvae prey upon soft-bodies garden pests, including caterpillars and aphids.

Lacewings and ladybugs can be encouraged to visit by planting candytuft, sunflowers, and marigolds.

LADYBUG

Predator

Despite their adorable appearance, ladybug (Lady Beetle) larvae are ferocious predators, feasting predominantly on aphids.

Plants that attract ladybugs include Angelica, Chives, Fennel, Statice, and Yarrow.
